Framework & Marginal Analysis

  • Social Welfare Maximization: Optimal policy occurs where MB = MC.
  • MB Declines: Marginal Benefit of prison declines as population grows (society incarcerates the most dangerous offenders first).
  • If MC > MB → Over-incarceration (Reduce).
    If MB > MC → Under-incarceration (Increase).
  • Policy Question is Marginal: Not "Is prison good?" but "Does incarcerating the next person help?"
  • Sources of Policy Disagreement: Values (Normative), Data (Measurement differences), Causal Assumptions (Direction of policy effects).

Crime Measurement

  • UCR (Uniform Crime Reporting): Police data. Uses Hierarchy Rule (counts only most serious offense per incident), severely understating crime volume.
  • NIBRS: Replaced UCR. Detailed demographics, counts all offenses per incident. Created massive data gaps during the 2021 transition.
  • NCVS (Victimization Survey): Captures the Dark Figure of unreported crime. Excludes homicides, homeless, and children <12.
  • Homicide Primacy: Most studied crime because it is hard to hide, has a consistent definition, carries a huge VSL, and allows cross-country comparisons.
  • Clearance Rates: Homicide ~61.6% | Burglary & Auto ~13.5%.

Modeling Criminal Behavior

Becker Static Model (1968): One-time gamble.

E[Ucrime] = p × U(Y - f) + (1 - p) × U(Y)
  • p: Probability of getting caught.
  • Y: Payoff (monetary/psychic).
  • f: Penalty (fine, prison, stigma).
  • U(): Utility function (captures risk aversion).

Deterrence Levers:

  • ↑ p (Certainty): More police, surveillance, DNA databases.
  • ↑ L (Length): Higher time cost (Mandatory minimums).
  • ↑ f (Severity): Fines, civil disabilities, losing housing.
  • ↓ Y (Payoff): Disrupt stolen goods markets.

Lee & McCrary (2017) Dynamic Model: Tests Age of Majority (18). Probability of adult charge jumps from 20% to 65%, but arrest rate doesn't change. Implication: Young offenders have high discount rates (myopic/ignore future costs).

The Social Burden of Crime

  • Tangible Costs: Medical bills, lost wages, property damage.
  • Intangible Costs: Trauma, pain, fear. Often dwarf tangible costs in violent crimes.
  • Valuation Methods:
    • Bottom-up: Summing tangible + civil jury awards.
    • Contingent Valuation: Surveys of Willingness to Pay (WTP). Subject to hypothetical bias.
    • Hedonic Pricing: Real market transactions (e.g., wage premiums for risky jobs, housing prices).
Key Cost Statistics:
• VSL (Value of Statistical Life): ~$6.4M to $8M.
• Gross Cost of Crime: $3.2 Trillion/year.
• Net of Transfers: $1.65 Trillion/year (subtracts transfers where victim loss = offender gain, as it's not a net social loss).
• Public Spending: ~$350 Billion (police, courts).
• Private Spending: ~$300 Billion (security, alarms).

Causal Inference & Methods

ATE = E[ Yi(1) - Yi(0) ]
  • Fundamental Problem: Cannot observe both treatment Y(1) and counterfactual Y(0) for the same entity.
  • Selection Bias: Positive (highly motivated succeed anyway → overestimates effect). Negative (struggling participants join → underestimates effect).
  • SUTVA: Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption. Requires homogeneous treatment and no externalities/spillovers (no general equilibrium effects).

Criminal Legal System

  • Discretion: Shiftable power. Police choose stops/arrests; Prosecutors choose charges; Judges choose sentences.
  • Bail as Constrained Optimization: Judges minimize social costs of pre-trial release:
minb E[ Ccrime × P(new offense|b) +
Cflight × P(flight|b) + Cdetention × P(detained|b) ]
  • Plea Bargaining: >90% of cases end in pleas. Driven by defendants' risk aversion and the massive Trial Penalty (sentences at trial are far harsher).
  • Juvenile Incarceration (Aizer & Doyle): Used random judge assignment (IV). Incarcerating youth increases adult imprisonment by 20pp (62% increase) and drops graduation rates.

Policing: Staffing & Benefits

  • The Double Dividend (Chalfin 2025): 1% ↑ in police → significant ↓ in serious crime. Results in fewer serious arrests (because crimes are prevented).
  • Net Widening: The downside to Chalfin's dividend. While serious arrests fall, low-level "quality of life" arrests (liquor, traffic) rise drastically.
  • Hot Spots & Deterrence (Draca et al. 2011): London 7/7 bombings. Police surged then drawn down. Drawdown was vital to isolate police presence from psychological distress. Found 13% drop in susceptible crime.
  • Private Security Spillovers: Positive externalities (Lojack breaks up chop shops, helping everyone). Negative externalities (Visible alarms displace burglars to neighbors).

Policing: Costs & Disparities

  • Educational Collateral Costs (Ang 2020): Living within 0.5 miles of a police killing drops local HS GPAs by 0.08 SD and decreases graduation by 3.5%.
  • Civilian Cooperation (Ang 2025): After George Floyd, 911 calls dropped relative to ShotSpotter alerts → civilians lost trust and stopped reporting crime.
  • Ferguson Effect (Devi & Fryer): Viral violence + DOJ investigations lead to abrupt de-policing (drop in police activity), causing subsequent homicide spikes.
  • Commander Stops (Bacher-Hicks): Aggressive commanders increase stops and minor misdemeanor arrests, but have zero effect on felony crime rates.
Racial Disparities & Benchmarking:
The Problem: Comparing arrests/force to "people stopped" is biased because the decision to stop someone is already racially biased (Selection Bias).
Hoekstra & Sloan (2020): Solved benchmarking by using 911 dispatch (random officer race). Found White officers use force 50-60% more than Black officers on similar calls, entirely driven by minority neighborhoods.
